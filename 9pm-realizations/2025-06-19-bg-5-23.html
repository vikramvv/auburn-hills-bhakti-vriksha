Tolerating lust and anger through pure devotional love (priti)

Jai Sacinandana K·πõ·π£·πáa DƒÅsa explains that pure love for Krishna and His devotees is the most effective way to transform these urges, making spiritual life natural and joyful.

Key Concepts

The Goal: Tolerating Urges (BG 5.23)

The lecture introduces Bhagavad Gita 5.23, which defines a 'yukta' (yogi) and truly happy person as one who can tolerate the urges of lust (kƒÅma) and anger (krodha) before giving up the body. It stresses that this spiritual advancement must be achieved during one's lifetime, countering the misconception that perfection can be easily attained after death or through mere ritualistic initiation. True transformation of consciousness requires diligent effort while alive.

Examples:

  • Not after dying, I can't become perfect.
  • Consciousness cannot be so much transformed because it's very difficult to surrender when the body is fragile.

The 'How' (Abhidheya): Priti vs. Other Paths

While BG 5.23 states the 'what' (prayojana) ‚Äì tolerating lust and anger ‚Äì it doesn't detail the 'how' (abhidheya). The speaker argues that knowledge (jnana), discipline, or other yoga techniques are insufficient because knowledge can be easily covered by illusion. The only thing that cannot be covered is prema (pure love for Krishna). This love acts as the most potent defense, making it impossible for lust and anger to penetrate one's heart.

Examples:

  • Knowledge can be very easily covered, but what cannot be covered is prema, love for Krishna.
  • It's a very prayojana shloka... But how to do that is not dealt in this chapter.

The Supremacy of Priti (Love for Krishna)

Priti, or love for Krishna and His devotees, is presented as the ultimate method to conquer lust. It's not about forceful suppression, which is difficult and unsustainable, but about transformation. When one cultivates priti, Mahamaya (Krishna's illusory energy) loses its power to fuel lust. Mahamaya feels shy before Krishna and His pure love, effectively cutting off the 'fuel' for lust, causing it to die a natural death. This shift makes spiritual life a natural and joyful activity rather than a stressful struggle.

Examples:

  • Lust doesn't have an address in your heart.
  • Mahamaya feels shy before Krishna; she cannot come before Krishna.
  • You can have the best car but you don't have a fuel, you can't go anywhere.

Cultivating Priti: Hearing and Devotee Association

Priti is cultivated primarily by hearing about Krishna's name, form, qualities, and pastimes, as described in scriptures like the 'Krishna Book'. Hearing about His sweetness, how He enchants gopis, or His childhood mischief, helps to install Krishna's lotus feet within the heart, leaving no space for lust. For those who may not yet have a direct connection with Krishna, cultivating priti towards the spiritual master and devotees serves as the essential initial step and practical introduction to this divine love.

Examples:

  • Reading Krishna book and hearing the pastimes of Krishna.
  • Hearing about Krishna's sweetness, how the gopis get stunned, Krishna stealing butter.

Beyond Gross Urges: Conquering Subtle Desires

Even if lust and anger are somehow suppressed, other subtle but powerful 'vegams' (urges) remain, such as labha (gain), puja (adoration), and pratishta (prestige/renown). These desires for fame, appreciation, and respect can also lead to misery and are equally difficult to conquer through mere discipline. The lecture concludes that genuine, affectionate, and honest service to the Lord, rooted in priti, is the only way to conquer all such material desires permanently, extending this love to guru and devotees.

Examples:

  • The desire for labha, puja and pratishta, the desire for adoration, the desire for worship, the desire for respect.
  • These expectations create misery in one's heart.

Practical Applications

  • Cultivate priti (love) for Krishna and His devotees.
  • Understand the difference between 'prayojana' (goal) and 'abhidheya' (means).
  • Rely on Krishna's internal potency (priti), not just personal self-discipline.
  • Address subtle desires (gain, adoration, prestige) through genuine, selfless service.
