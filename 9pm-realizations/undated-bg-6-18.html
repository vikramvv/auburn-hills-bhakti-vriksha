Key Takeaway

A true yogi, or `yukta`, is one whose disciplined mind is fixed on the self or Supreme Self, having renounced all material desires arising from the egoistic urge to lord it over.

Main Theme

Regulating the mind by giving up lordship and material desires

Summary

This lecture delves into Bhagavad Gita 6.18, defining a true yogi (`yukta`) as one who, through disciplined practice, fixes their mind on the self and relinquishes the desire to control. By shedding the ego's drive for lordship, one finds inner peace and purpose in serving Krishna.

Key Concepts

Defining a Yogi (Yukta) in BG 6.18

The lecture begins by explaining the characteristics of a `yukta`, a person well-established in yoga, according to Bhagavad Gita 6.18. This state is achieved when the mind (`chittam`) is completely regulated (`viniyatam`) and fixed on the self or Super-soul (`atmanya eva avatishthate`). Crucially, a `yukta` is devoid of all material desires (`nishpraha sarvakame bhyo`), distinguishing them from ordinary individuals. The cessation of material desires, particularly the desire for sense gratification, marks the true yogi.

Examples:

  • Cessation from all kinds of material desires, of which sex is the chief (as per Prabhupada's purport)
  • Ambarish Maharaj as an example of a fixed yogi.

The Significance of 'Eva Shabdham'

The speaker highlights the recurring 'eva' (`only` or `certainly`) in Krishna's teachings, emphasizing exclusivity in spiritual life. While material life is bifurcated and diversified by many pursuits, spiritual life demands singular focus. This 'eva shabdham' points to the mind's fixed state, where it does not desire to go anywhere else. This exclusivity is key to regulating the mind and achieving the state of a `yukta`.

Examples:

  • Krishna's use of 'eva' in 'maam eva ye prapadya' (only those who surrender to Me)
  • 'Atmaratir eva syad atmatih tastam anava' (one who finds pleasure in the self alone).

The Innate Desire to Lord It Over

A profound insight shared is that the fundamental cause of mental distraction and agitation is the soul's innate, albeit false, tendency to 'lord it over.' This desire manifests in myriad ways, such as wanting to be the best, possess the most, or control others. When one lacks the power to directly lord, they often outsource this tendency to possessions or loved ones (e.g., parents wanting their children to excel). This false ego is the root of material desires and subsequent anxieties.

Examples:

  • Wanting to be better than a colleague, the best speaker, have the best spouse or child.
  • Indian parents pushing their children in competitions, fulfilling their own unachieved desires.

Anxiety and Distraction from False Lordship

The desire to lord it over leads directly to anxiety and distraction. The mind is constantly agitated, scanning the environment for opportunities to assert control or superiority. When this desire is relinquished, and one accepts the position of a servant of Krishna, there is no longer any reason for anxiety. The external world ceases to be a battleground for egoistic pursuits and instead serves a singular purpose: to facilitate service to the Lord.

Examples:

  • A confused person not knowing 'where to lord' due to problems, anxieties, and competition.
  • Seeing a gold coin and a stone as having the same significance if both are seen in relation to serving Krishna.

Unified Vision and Mind Regulation through Servanthood

When the consciousness gives up the tendency to lord it over and agrees to be the servant of Krishna, the mind automatically becomes regulated (`viniyatam chittam`). This happens because the distinction between inside and outside dissolves; every object and every aspect of the world is then seen as serving only one purpose ‚Äì to serve the Lord. This unified vision eliminates the mind's tendency to wander externally in search of diverse material achievements, as all desired achievements are found internally in devotional service (`krishna-kama`).

Examples:

  • Knowing only that Zimbabwe is a country in Africa, without needing exhaustive detail, because one has no purpose to achieve there.
  • Realizing that whatever can be achieved outside can be achieved better inside (in Krishna consciousness).

Practical Applications

  • Identify and transcend the desire to lord it over.
  • Cultivate a service attitude towards all objects and beings.
  • Regulate the mind by fixing it on Krishna's service.
  • Embrace exclusivity in spiritual focus.
