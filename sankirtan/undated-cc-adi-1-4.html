Divine Strategy: Mastering Book Distribution for Krishna Bhakti

Key Takeaway

This lecture illuminates how engaging in book distribution, following Prabhupada's methods, is not only the highest form of devotional service but also a direct path to experiencing transcendental ecstasy, pleasing Krishna, and offering unparalleled mercy to fallen souls.

Summary

Learn practical, Prabhupada-approved techniques for effective book distribution, from opening conversations to closing sales. Discover how this essential service brings profound spiritual benefits, pleases Krishna, and elevates both the distributor and the recipient to higher consciousness.

Key Concepts

The Unprecedented Mercy of Lord Chaitanya

The lecture begins by highlighting the unique and unparalleled mercy of Lord Chaitanya, as described in CC Adi 1.4. In the age of Kali, Lord Chaitanya has appeared to bestow the 'unnatojjvala-rasam' ‚Äî the most sublime and radiant mellow of devotional service, specifically conjugal love. This highest spiritual gift, never before offered by any incarnation, is made accessible through the Sankirtan movement, offering incredible potential for spiritual elevation even to the most fallen souls of Kali Yuga.

Book Distribution as Supreme Devotional Service

Book distribution is presented as the most potent form of devotional service, bringing direct contact with Krishna's internal potency for hours on end. It offers immense spiritual benefit to the distributor, leading to an experience of 'swimming in ecstasy.' For the recipients, it provides their first contact with Krishna consciousness, planting the seed for their spiritual journey, regardless of their prior qualifications or understanding. This service offers mercy far beyond traditional eligibility criteria, such as those mentioned in Bhagavad Gita.

Foundation of Devotional Practice

Srila Prabhupada emphasized that the 'real technique' for book distribution lies in a strong spiritual foundation. This means being a devout follower of the rules and regulations: chanting 16 rounds daily, adhering to the four regulative principles, and regularly studying Prabhupada's books. This foundation is likened to the critical 50-70% of work that goes into building a solid structure, ensuring that one's efforts in book distribution are stable and sustainable.

Psychology of Engagement: Flattery & Tactics

The lecture delves into practical techniques for engaging people, including the strategic use of flattery and 'tactics,' sanctioned by Prabhupada and predecessor Acharyas like Prabodhananda Saraswati. This involves giving sincere compliments to break down initial resistance and create an opening for conversation. Prabhupada clarified that such methods are not 'cheating' but necessary tactics when dealing with worldly people, to achieve the higher goal of bringing them to Krishna consciousness, as per Rupa Goswami's instruction: 'Yena teyna prakareyna manakrishnae nivesayat' (somehow or other engage the mind in Krishna).

The Mood of the Gopis in Sankirtan

The speaker explains how book distribution aligns with the mood of the gopis. Just as the gopis' sole desire is to please Krishna, book distributors engage in this service to fulfill Krishna's desire for fallen souls to receive His mercy and return to Him. This means being prepared to undergo austerities, like enduring cold weather or dealing with rude individuals, because it is for Krishna's satisfaction. This understanding elevates the service beyond mere obligation to a deeply loving exchange.

Discernment & Persistence in Distribution

Effective book distribution requires discernment, knowing when and how to engage with different individuals. The 'ripe fruit' analogy teaches distributors to identify receptive people who show little resistance and are eager to receive books, focusing energy on them. Conversely, it advises against wasting excessive time on 'unripe' or 'rotten' fruits (highly resistant or uninterested individuals). While persistence is encouraged through a few objections, knowing when to gracefully move on is crucial for efficiency and maintaining one's own enthusiasm.

Spiritual Protection & Success

To overcome the inevitable impediments and objections encountered in preaching, the speaker strongly recommends seeking the mercy of the Six Goswamis. He shares his personal experience of chanting the Six Goswamis' prayers during a difficult book distribution marathon, which transformed his mood and led to success. Prabhupada's purport to CC Antya 1.3-4 reinforces that taking shelter of the merciful Goswamis ensures protection and success in the mission of spreading Krishna consciousness.

Self-Realization through Service

Prabhupada affirmed that those engaged in strenuous book distribution are 'already self-realized.' He explained that their love for Krishna manifests in their willingness to labor hard to establish Krishna as the Supreme Lord. This dedication to spreading God consciousness is a direct indicator of one's spiritual advancement, satisfying Krishna, and fulfilling the devotee's purpose without seeking personal remuneration or even a specific destination like Vaikuntha.

If we show a tower of Srimad-Bhagavatam and Chaitanya Charitamrita, and people say, 'Hey, there's so many books, I cannot take it,' what should be our point?
The speaker admits he doesn't have a perfect line for this, as students in his area don't often take full sets. He emphasizes that it's easier to get people to give money than to take many books. His strategy is to focus on getting a good donation first for a few softcover books, and then, if they are generous, offer more books, even a second set for a friend, making them feel they received good value. The goal is to maximize book distribution, not just money.
Sometimes, I feel like a person is very interested and seems like 'ripe fruit,' but after a few minutes, when I show them the Bhagavatam, they back off completely. What happened?
The speaker states, 'You just talked yourself out of a sale. You got to keep it short.' He emphasizes that book distribution is like 'martial arts' ‚Äì a focused, sequential process (compliment, qualifying question, small book intro, compliment, close). Casual conversation gives them time to think how to escape. Maintain control and focus on the steps without lingering.
How do we handle situations where people, like Christian missionaries or atheists, try to engage us in lengthy arguments and take up a lot of time? When do we stop and move on?
The speaker advises not to get drawn into debates. If they are not willing to take a book, politely state that you are focused on distributing books to many people. Suggest they come back later (when you'll be gone). For Christians, he suggests a brief mention of being 'real Christians' and showing sections about Christ in Prabhupada's books, but not engaging in theological arguments. The core advice is to stay focused on selling books and excuse oneself gracefully if the conversation deviates.
In malls, people often say, 'I just came for shopping, I'll come back later' or 'I don't have time.' How do we respond to this?
The speaker recommends being 'pushy' but in a charming, smiling way. Insist on doing it 'now.' Use the 'force of your personality' to overcome their excuses. If they say they don't want to carry it, offer to hold it or put it in their car. The key is to be more determined to make the sale than they are to avoid it, often with jokes and smiles.
Towards the end of the year, people say, 'I'm out of budget, I can't spend anything more.' How to handle this?
For those claiming poverty, the speaker suggests agreeing with them initially and then offering a 'small donation.' The goal is to get them to agree to something. He recommends using a line like, 'Typically people give that [show a small number like $20], but you can give more, you can give less.' He notes that people often give more than they initially claimed they could. The devotee's desire and Krishna's influence from within the heart play a role here. Don't let them get away too easily.
When doing Sankirtan for many hours, how do I prevent the interactions (conversations, rejections) from affecting my chanting (japa) and overall Krishna consciousness the next day?
The speaker suggests immediately engaging in hearing Prabhupada's lectures or kirtan after Sankirtan to re-absorb oneself in transcendental sound. He notes that while on Sankirtan, one is in a transcendental service, but afterwards, it's crucial to take shelter again. If intrusive thoughts come during chanting, acknowledge them but continue with the chanting, knowing you did your best in service.
Sometimes people are interested but hesitate at the price or say, 'I have many books to read, I'm busy.' How to handle this?
Continue to offer compliments to soften them. For 'too many books,' ask them to take 'just this one book' and be nonchalant about the donation, making it seem easy. Reiterate that the donation helps a cause (distributing books to students nationwide). Persist charmingly through two or three objections, offering a free book if they remain unwilling, but give them a 'run for their money.'
